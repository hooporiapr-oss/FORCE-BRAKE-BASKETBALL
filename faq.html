What is flywheel training?

It is a resistance method where the athlete’s effort helps shape the training demand, creating a stronger relationship between output and control.

Why does braking matter in basketball?

Because cuts, closeouts, counters, landings, recoveries, and shot preparation all depend on how well the athlete can decelerate into position.

How is this different from traditional lifting?

It puts a stronger spotlight on the connection between producing force and then controlling the return.

Is this only about strength?

No. It is about usable force, body organization, movement control, and the quality of what happens after the first action.

Can this support change of direction?

Yes. Better redirection starts with better braking.

Does this only apply to advanced athletes?

No. The principles matter across levels, and the method can scale while still teaching force awareness and movement discipline.

Can it fit into basketball performance work year-round?

Yes. It can support off-season development, in-season maintenance, and long-term movement education when programmed with clarity.

How does force transfer to actual basketball movement?

Force matters when it shows up in first steps, stance quality, contact finishes, vertical actions, landings, and repeated movement under pressure.

Why is stopping such a big deal in basketball?

Because the stop decides what the body can do next. Good braking protects balance, timing, posture, and the quality of re-acceleration.
